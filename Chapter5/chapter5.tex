In this chapter we demonstrate how a certain control problem that is known to be 
NP-hard for unrestricted preferences, becomes polynomial-time solvable for the VCR elections.
First in Section \ref{ch5:sec1:overview}, we discuss variants of the control problem. 
Additionally, we present two existing polynomial-time algorithms for a control problem, for elections in the VR and the CR domain.
Later in Section \ref{ch5:sec2:vcr-control}, we describe our solution for the VCR elections,
and we show that it unifies the two aforementioned algorithms for the VR and the CR cases.

\section{Control (WIP: Inny tytuł?)}\label{ch5:sec1:overview}
Control problems were first introduced by \cite{bartholdi-control}, and later extended by \cite{hemaspaandra-control}.
The control refers to an act of manipulating the structure of an election in a certain way.
We assume that there is some person or authority who is in charge of organizing the election.
We refer to them as the \textit{election chair}.
The chair has a goal to change the outcome of the election in a certain way.
Formally we call it the \textit{control type},
and we distinguish two types. The first one, the \textit{constructive} control (CC), 
where the chair's goal is to ensure a victory of a selected candidate.
The second one, the \textit{destructive} control (DC), which is an opposite,
that is, the chair's intention is to prevent a selected candidate from winning.
Furthermore, control problems are grouped by the method used to change the structure of an election, we call it the \textit{control action}.
Usually in literature, for each control type (i.e, the CC or the DC) we consider four possible actions, that is,
adding candidates (AC), deleting candidates (DC), adding voters (AV) or deleting voters (DV).
For further reading, see the chapter by \cite{control-hcsc-faliszewski},
which provides a complete overview of the control problem and the closely related bribery problem as well.

The algorithms that we discuss in the following sections, consider the constructive control by deleting voters (CC-DV),
and of course, for the winner determination, we use the approval voting rule (see Definition \ref{ch2:def:av}).
Below, we give a formal definition of our problem:
\begin{definition}\label{ch5:def:av-cc-dv}
    We are given a set of candidates $C$, a set of voters $V$, a nonnegative integer k, and a selected candidate $p \in C$.
    In the constructive control by deleting voters problem for the approval voting (Approval-CC-DV),
    we ask whether there exists a set $V^{\prime} \subseteq V$,
    such that $p$ is the unique winner of the approval voting election $E = (C, V \setminus V^{\prime})$, where $|V^{\prime}| \leq k$.
\end{definition}

\noindent Now, we define some basic concepts and terminology, that will help us to explain the algorithms in the subsequent sections.
First, we introduce an alternative representation of an agent in a VCR profile.
\begin{definition}\label{ch5:def:vcr-range-notation}
    In the VCR model, every agent $a$ is represented by the point $x_a$ and the radius $r_a$.
    We define $a$'s range beginning and range end as: \[b(a) = x_a - r_a,\] \[e(a) = x_a + r_a.\]
\end{definition}
\noindent Additionally, to represent the AV-score difference between two candidates, we use:
\begin{definition}\label{ch5:def:av-score-notation}
    Having an approval election $E$ and two candidates $a$ and $b$. 
    Under the approval-voting scoring rule, we say that:
    \[scoreDelta(E,a,b) = score_{AV}(E,a) - score_{AV}(E,b).\]
\end{definition}
\noindent However, we note that we use a more concise notation, that is, the $scoreDelta(a,b)$.

\subsection{Constructive Control by Deleting Voters for the VR Elections}\label{ch5:sec:1:cc-dv-vr}
This algorithm is presented in the work of \cite{control-sp-shield-faliszewski-hemaspaandra}.
It is the first paper to consider solving control problems under preference restrictions.
The restriction that they consider is the single-peaked one.
However, in the approval elections it is virtually identical to the voter range model (i.e., candidates' radii are zero).
We only present a brief description of the algorithm, that explains the core idea and some necessary concepts.
For the detailed explanation, proofs and examples we point to the source work.

\begin{figure}[t!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{vr-ref-ccvd.png}
    \caption{Illustration of the CC-DV for the VR elections from \cite{control-sp-shield-faliszewski-hemaspaandra}.
    Bar chart of approvals from $V$. The candidates are in the VR order,
    and each candidate is labeled with respect to whether he or she is left or right
    of the candidate $p$. Additionally, the “dangerous” candidates are marked with shaded bars.
    }
    \label{fig:vr-ccdv-ref}
\end{figure}

The algorithm deploys a greedy strategy. In the explanation we follow the example from Figure~\ref{fig:vr-ccdv-ref}.
Let us consider a problem of the CC-VD under the approval voting rule.
Having a set of candidates $C$ in the VR order, a set of voters $V$,
a designated winner candidate $p$, and a maximum number of voter deletions $k$.
To find a solution (if it exists) we take the following steps:
First, we group the candidates based on their position relative to $p$, i.e., left or right from $p$.
Then, we proceeded to selecting the dangerous candidates (opponents). The first opponent to the right of $p$
is the leftmost candidate from the right group, that gets at lest as many approvals as $p$ (i.e., $r_3$). 
Every next right dangerous candidate is the candidate
that gets strictly more approvals than the previous dangerous candidate (i.e., $r_6$, and then $r_{10}$).
We take analogous steps for the candidates on the left. 
From this point, we try to defeat dangerous candidates. We process one at a time in each iteration.
We start with the closest right opponent (i.e., $r_3$), and call him $c_R$. Now we have to choose which and how many voters to delete.
We consider only a subset of voters, such that each voters' range beginning is in the open-closed interval between $p$ and $c_R$,
i.e., $\widehat{V} = \{v \in V | \: b(v) \in (p,c_R]\}$.
Next we try to delete $scoreDelta(c_R,p) + 1$ rightmost voters from $\widehat{V}$, that is the ones with the highest range end (i.e., $e(v)$).
If we fail, or we exceed the voter deletion limit $k$, the solution does not exist.
We repeat these steps until the candidate $p$ becomes a unique winner (or we fail in the process).
We recalculate the approvals and the dangerous candidates set after each iteration.
Once we reached the point where all the dangerous candidates are in the left group, then
we simply “flip the universe”, and consider them to be the right ones.


\subsection{Constructive Control by Deleting Voters for the CR Elections}\label{ch5:sec:1:cc-dv-cr}
% \subsection{AV-CC-DV for Candidate Range = VI = column C1P = $r_v=0$}

The next algorithm is due to \cite{control-cr-how-hard-magiera-faliszewski}.
They study multiple election types, but we will discuss only the approval elections case.
They consider control problems for the single-crossing preferences restriction.
Which, in the approval elections, is virtually identical to the candidate range model (i.e., voters' radii are zero).
We again provide only a brief explanation of the algorithm, and for specifics we refer to the original paper. 
This algorithm has many similarities to the previous one, mainly it utilizes a greedy strategy as well.
Let us consider a problem of the CC-VD under the approval voting rule.
Having a set of candidates $C$, a set of voters $V$ in the CR order,
a designated winner candidate $p$, and a maximum number of voter deletions $k$.
For each candidate $c \in C$, we say that the voters who approve of $c$ form an interval: $v_{s(c)}, v_{s(c)+1}, ..., v_{f(c)}$.
Additionally, we define a set of dangerous candidates: $\widetilde{C} = \{c \in C \setminus \{p\} | \: scoreDelta(c,p) \geq 0\}$,
and a subset of voters who do not approve of $p$: $V^{\prime} = \{v \in V | \: p \notin A(v)\}$.
The rest of the algorithm is straightforward.
It executes the following operation until $p$ is a winner, or we fail by reaching the limit of deletions $k$.
First, we need to select a dangerous candidate that we will try to defeat.
To do so, we find an opponent $c_N \in \widetilde{C}$, for whom $f(c_N)$ is the smallest.
Then, we try to delete $scoreDelta(c_N, p) + 1$ voters, starting with the voter at the end of interval (i.e., $v_{f(c_N)}$).
If we fail, the solution does not exist. After each iteration of this step, we recalculate the sets $\widetilde{C}$ and $V^{\prime}$.

\section{Constructive Control by Deleting Voters for the VCR Elections}\label{ch5:sec2:vcr-control}

This time, we describe our algorithm in full, and consider all the caveats.
For the sake of completeness, we will define some notions once again.

Let us consider a problem of CC-VD under the approval voting rule.
Having a VCR election, with a set of candidates $C$, a set of voters $V$,
a designated winner candidate $p$, and a maximum number of voter deletions $k$.
First, we discuss some concepts and obvious observations, that will help us explain the algorithm afterwards.
We use an alternative representation of an agent in the VCR election,
that is, by his or hers range beginning and end (see Definition \ref{ch5:def:vcr-range-notation}).
Next, for $p$ to become a unique winner, we need to defeat every candidate that has at least as many votes as $p$.
We call this subset of candidates a set of dangerous candidates (opponents),
formally: \[\widetilde{C} = \{c \in C \setminus \{p\} | \: scoreDelta(c,p) \geq 0\}.\]
Another observation is that we never delete a voter who approves of $p$,
clearly it would not bring us any closer to a solution.
When deleting voters, we consider only the ones from a set: \[V^{\prime} = \{v \in V | \: p \notin A(v)\}.\]  

\begin{figure}[ht!]
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{vcr-example.png}
    \centering
    \caption{A VCR election example for the CC-VD problem.
    The candidates are labeled with $C_i$ (expect for the preferred candidate $P$), and the voters with $V_i$.
    We use colors to distinguish some key groups of agents. 
    That is, $P$ is in dark-blue, and voters that approve of him or her (we never delete them) are in light-blue color.
    Additionally, all the initially dangerous candidates are in red.
    }
    \label{fig:cc-dv-vcr}
\end{figure}

Our algorithm is a greedy one.
It repeats the following steps until $p$ becomes a unique winner,
or it fails by exceeding the maximum number of voter deletions $k$.
First, we find a dangerous candidate that we will try to defeat. 
Then, to achieve this goal, we decide which voters to delete.
After each iteration, we recompute the set of dangerous candidates $\widetilde{C}$ and the set of selected voters $V^{\prime}$.
We describe these steps in detail below:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Choosing opponents.} 
    The only candidates that we consider are from the set of opponents $\widetilde{C}$.
    We pick the leftmost candidate, that is, the one with the minimal beginning of the range $b(c)$ (we break ties arbitrarily).
    We refer to the selected candidate as $c_L$.

    \item \textbf{Deleting voters.}
    Having currently processed opponent $c_L$.
    We consider only the voters who approve of him or her,
    i.e., $\widehat{V} = \{v \in V' | \: c_L \in A(v)\}$.
    To defeat $c_L$, we need to delete $scoreDelta(c_L,p) + 1$ voters from $\widehat{V}$.
    If $|\widehat{V}| < scoreDelta(c_L,p) + 1$, then we fail (it is impossible to delete enough voters to defeat $c_L$).
    We proceed with deleting the rightmost voters, that is, the ones with the maximum end of the range $e(v)$ (we break ties arbitrarily).
    By choosing the rightmost voters, we try to maximize the number of other dangerous candidates who might be contained inside each voter's range.
    We can do so because of our leftmost candidate picking strategy.
    Any dangerous candidate to the left of $c_L$ would have already been defeated.    
\end{enumerate}
We illustrate the algorithm in Example \ref{ex:cc-dv-vcr}, which describes an election from Figure \ref{fig:cc-dv-vcr}.

\begin{example}\label{ex:cc-dv-vcr}
    Let us discuss an instance of the CC-VD problem. Having the VCR election from Figure \ref{fig:cc-dv-vcr},
    the designated candidate $P$ with 3 votes (i.e., $\{V_6, V_7, V_8\}$) and
    the set of dangerous candidates $\widetilde{C} = \{C_2, C_3, C_4\}$.
    We proceed to find the smallest number of voters that we need to delete for $P$ to win the election.
    First, we choose the leftmost dangerous candidate $C_2$.
    We need to delete 2 voters ($scoreDelta(C_2,P) + 1$). 
    We pick the rightmost voters $V_4$ and $V_3$.
    Now, an important observation is that by removing $V_4$ we defeated next dangerous candidate $C_3$.
    We finish this iteration, and recompute sets $\widetilde{C}$ and $V^{\prime}$.
    The only left dangerous candidate is $C_4$, and we just need to delete 1 voter. 
    We pick $V_10$ (in this case it wouldn't matter if we chose $V_9$).

\end{example}

Later, we provide informal sketch proofs that show how our algorithm is equivalent to the VR and the CR ones.
Finally, we give a formal proof of correctness of the algorithm.

\subsection{Special Case of Contained Candidates}

In the VCR model it is possible that some candidates have their interval fully contained in the other candidate's interval. 
If so, every voter who would approve the smaller candidate (i.e., internal) would also approve the larger one (i.e., external),
however not vice versa. For this case, our algorithm's strategy of choosing rightmost voters to delete, might seem suboptimal at first glance. 
We define additional terminology and symbols to help us explain why we do not have to worry about this special case. 

\begin{definition}\label{ch5:def:delta-notation}
    Having an instance of the CC-VD problem, with an approval election $E$, a set of candidates $C$, and the designated winner candidate $p$.
    We extend representation of the score difference between two candidates (see Definition \ref{ch5:def:av-score-notation}).
    For each candidate $c \in C \setminus \{p\}$ we represent a number of voter deletions required for $p$ to defeat $c$ with:
    \[\Delta(c) = score_{AV}(E,c) - score_{AV}(E,p) + 1.\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}\label{ch5:def:contained-vi-ve}
    Having an election $E$, where a candidate is contained inside another one.
    The two candidates are, the one with larger range $c_E$, and the one with smaller range $c_I$.
    The voters who approve of $c_E$ or $c_I$ are in a set $\widehat{V}$.
    We divide the voters into two groups. First, a subset of voters who approve of $c_E$ but not $c_I$:
    \[\widehat{V}_E = \{v \in \widehat{V} \: | \: c_E \in A(v) \land c_I \notin A(V)\},\]
    and the remaining ones:
    \[\widehat{V}_I = \widehat{V} \setminus \widehat{V}_E.\]
    We know the size of these subsets based on the approval scores of each candidate:
    \[|\widehat{V}_E| = score_{AV}(E, c_E) - score_{AV}(E, c_I),\]
    \[|\widehat{V}_I| = |\widehat{V}| - |\widehat{V}_E|.\]

\end{definition}

Let us consider an instance of the CC-VD problem, with an election that has the special case.
We know that a contained candidate $c_I$ has as much or less total votes than the larger candidate $c_E$ (i.e., $|\widehat{V}| \geq |\widehat{V}_I|$).
The internal candidate $c_I$ might not even be a dangerous candidate at all. 
For $p$ to defeat $c_E$ we need to delete $\Delta(c_E)$ voters,
and to defeat $c_I$ (independently of $c_E$), we need to delete $\Delta(c_I)$ voters (see Definition \ref{ch5:def:delta-notation}).
Now we show that in the process of defeating the larger candidate we always automatically defeat the smaller one.

\begin{equation}
    \label{ch5:eq:contained:transf}
    \begin{split}
        \Delta(c_E) =\\
        score_{AV}(c_E) - score_{AV}(p) + 1 =\\
        |\widehat{V}_E| + score_{AV}(c_I) - score_{AV}(p) + 1 =\\
        |\widehat{V}_E| + \Delta(c_I).
    \end{split}    
\end{equation}
Using simple transformations in Equation \ref{ch5:eq:contained:transf} based on Definition \ref{ch5:def:contained-vi-ve},
we show that: \[\Delta(c_E) = |\widehat{V}_E| + \Delta(c_I).\]
It means that to defeat $c_E$ we need to delete at least $\Delta(c_I)$ voters from $\widehat{V}_I$,
the remaining $|\widehat{V}_E|$ voters can be deleted from any of the subsets (i.e., $\widehat{V}_E$ or $\widehat{V}_I$).
This will result in defeating both $c_E$ and $c_I$ (automatically).

\subsubsection{Choosing opponents}
In the CR algorithm, we choose candidates to process, ordered by $v_{e(c)}$ ascending.
We can see that it is equivalent to picking the leftmost ($min(b(c))$) candidate from the set of opponents $\widehat{C}$ in the VCR algorithm,
because it will also have the minimal $v_{e(c)}$.

There is a special case of "contained candidate", for which the above statement is not true.
In Section \ref{sss:contained-candidate}, we explain in detail why it is irrelevant for the overall correctness of the algorithm.

\subsubsection{Voter deletion}
Now, once we now how to pick an opponent $c_L$, we will show that choosing voters is equivalent as well.
Notion of the beginning/end of the interval in the CR order is equivalent to the left/right end of the range in the VCR setting.
Both algorithms try to delete $scoreDelta(c_L,p) + 1$ voters,
CR one starts deleting voters from the end of interval, while
VCR does virtually the same by deleting the rightmost voter first ($max(e(v))$).

\subsubsection{Special case - contained candidates} \label{sss:contained-candidate}
In the VCR model it is possible that some candidates have their interval fully contained in the other's interval. 
Every voter who would approve the smaller candidate would also approve the larger one, however not vice versa.
For this case, our strategy of choosing voters to delete might seem suboptimal, at first glance. 

The example from Figure \ref{fig:contained-candidate} illustrates why we do not have to worry about this special case.
Every contained voter will have as much or less total votes than the larger candidate, 
he/she might not even be a dangerous candidate at all. 
Now we see that in the process of defeating the larger candidate we will automatically defeat the smaller one.

\subsubsection{Choosing opponents}
We consider only dangerous candidates (opponents), that is the ones with score higher or equal to the score of $p$.
We process opponents one by one, starting with the one who has the leftmost beginning of the range, i.e., $min(b(c))$.
After each iteration, we recompute the set of dangerous candidates.
Thanks to this we are sure that all the steps we perform are necessary to find a solution (if it exists), hence optimal.

\subsubsection{Voter deletion}
Having currently processed opponent $c_L$, we consider only the voters who approve of him/her and do not approve of $p$,
that is the voters whose range beginning is inside the candidate's range $\{v \in V' \: | \: b(v) \in [b(c_L), e(c_L)]\}$.
We need to delete $scoreDelta(c_L,p) + 1$ voters to defeat the opponent.
By picking the voters in order of the rightmost end of the range, i.e., $v_R = max(e(v))$,
we try to maximize the number of other candidates who might be in the range $[b(v_R), e(v_R)]$.
We don't have to worry about any candidate to the left,
because of our leftmost candidate picking strategy, they are already defeated.
This strategy ensures the solution is optimal if it exists.

\subsection{Constructive Control by Adding Voters for the VCR Elections}
Our strategy is based on the solution from \cite{control-cr-how-hard-magiera-faliszewski}.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Add all $v \in W$ to the election
    \item Is $p$ the winner?
    \item Try to delete $k' = |W| - k$ voters 
\end{enumerate}
