\section{How many VCR profiles are not VR neither CR?}

\subsection{Experiment 1 — Small Profiles}
For the small profiles we did a complete search of the domain.
That is we generated every possible profile of a given size and checked if it has the VCR property.
We checked 6 dimensions of the elections with the following sizes, having $M$ candidates and $N$ voters,
$(M,N) \in \{(3,3), (4,4), (5,5), (4,6), (6,4)\}$.
The results as seen in Figure \ref{fig:small-statistics-barchart} show that
VCR domain is relatively large, however only limited number of profiles

\subsection{Experiment 2 — Large Profiles}
For the larger size of the profiles we generated a suitable number of random VCR profiles
and again checked how many of them are neither CR nor VR (\textit{NCOP}), and additionally, VR and CR (\textit{COP}),
VR and not CR, CR and not VR.
We employed several strategies for generating the profiles. 
The first parameterized category is a statistical distribution used to pick the points representing voters and candidates.
The second one is the choice of radius parameter. We describe the categories in detail below.
For each parameter pair we generated 1000 elections with 20 candidates and 20 voters,
then we repeated the experiment for the profiles of 40 candidates and 40 voters.
 

\subsubsection{Position Parameter}
We consider 4 ways of generating the points, representing the positions of the candidates and the voters.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The \textit{Uniform Candidates, Uniform Voters} model,
    where the points for both, the candidates and the voters are selected uniformly at random from the $[-10,10]$ range.
    
    \item The \textit{Asymmetric Gauss Candidates, Asymmetric Gauss Voters} model,
    where we take 70\% of points from a Gaussian distribution with mean value $-3$ and standard deviation $1.8$,
    and the remaining 30\% come from a Gaussian with mean $3$ and the same standard deviation. 
    This model is inspired by the 2 dimensional model from the work of \cite{falisz}. 
    The goal is to simulate the society with a large majority, and a minority with the opposite opinion. 

    \item The \textit{Uniform Candidates, Asymmetric Gauss Voters} model,
    it is a combination of the previous two models, where
    the candidate's points are selected uniformly at random,
    and for the voters we use asymmetric Gaussian model.
    We use the same parameters for both of the distributions.

    \item The \textit{Asymmetric Gauss Candidates, Uniform Voters} model,
    it is mirror version of the previous model, with the distributions swapped.

\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Radius Parameter}
In this category we consider 5 ways of generating the radii.
We split the methods between the candidates and the voters, similarly to picking the positions.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item\label{enum:radius-gaus-gaus} The \textit{Gauss Candidates, Gauss Voters} model, 
    where we choose the radii from the Gaussian distribution with mean value $1.5$ and standard deviation $0.5$
    We use the same distribution for the candidates and the voters.

    \item The \textit{Small Uniform Candidates, Gauss Voters} model.
    We pick radii for the candidates uniformly at random from the range $[0.7,1.2]$,
    and for the voters we use the same Gaussian, as in the previous model ($Gauss(1.5, 0.5)$).

    \item The \textit{Gauss Candidates, Small Uniform Voters} model.
    In this model we swap the distributions used in the previous one,
    that is, the candidates are picked from the Gaussian and the voters from the uniform distributions.

    \item\label{enum:suc-suv} The \textit{Small Uniform Candidates, Small Uniform Voters} model.
    We pick radii for both the candidates and the voters, uniformly at random from the range $[0.7,1.2]$.

    \item The \textit{Large Uniform Candidates, Large Uniform Voters} model.
    We pick radii for both the candidates and the voters, uniformly at random from the range $[0,3]$.

\end{enumerate}

At first, it might be surprising why we don't include a constant radius, as one of the parameters.
That is because, then all the profiles would be VR and CR (\textit{COP}),
as we show in Proposition \ref{prop:vcr-constR-vrcr}.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:vcr-constR-vrcr}
    If a profile $P$ satisfies VCR and every agent (candidate/voter) in the profile, has the same constant radius, then
    $P$ satisfies both VR and CR properties.

\section{Transforming VCR (NCOP) Profile into VR or CR}
A natural question that arises from the previous experiment results, 
is how different are the NCOP profiles from the VR or CR ones? 
In other words, how much do we need to change a NCOP profile for it to become a VR / CR one. 
To answer this question, we design another experiment.

\subsection{Experiment 3 — Profile Transformation}
In this experiment, we partially reuse profiles generated for the previous one.
We narrow down our experiment parameters to only two radius generation methods,
that is, first model \textit{Gauss Candidates, Gauss Voters} and second \textit{Large Uniform Candidates, Large Uniform Voters}.
We keep the position models unchanged, and we explore all four of them.
Obviously we only pick the NCOP profiles, to be exact, we use 400 of them for each parameter pair.
\newline

\noindent \textbf{Agent Deletion Parameter} \indent We consider 3 methods to transform a NCOP profile into a VR / CR one.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The \textit{Candidate Deletion} method, 
    where we search for a minimal number $d$,
    such that, after deleting $d$ candidates from the profile it becomes a VR / CR profile.

    \item The \textit{Voter Deletion} method, 
    which is similar to the \textit{Candidate Deletion} method, but this time we delete $d$ voters instead.

    \item The \textit{Candidate and Voter Deletion} method, 
    where we combine the two previous methods, and we simultaneously delete $d_c$ candidates and $d_v$ voters.
\end{enumerate}


\noindent \textbf{Transformation Result Parameter} \indent Apart from choosing what type of agent (candidate / voter) to delete,
for each profile we collect the transformation results into 3 groups.
By the transformation result we mean the lowest possible cost of the transformation,
i.e., the minimal number of agent deletions for a TVCR profile to become a VR / CR one.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The \textit{Candidate Range}, where the cost is a number of deletions required to get a CR profile.
    \item The \textit{Voter Range}, where the cost is a number of deletions required to get a VR profile.
    \item The \textit{Candidate Range or Voter Range}, where the cost is a minimum from the previous two groups. 
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Results}
