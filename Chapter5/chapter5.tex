\subsection{AV-CC-DV for Single Peaked ~(VR = CI = row C1P = $r_c=0$)}

Algorithm from \cite{control-sp-shield-faliszewski-hemaspaandra}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item kandydat $p$ na osi, pozostali na lewo i prawo
    \item wybór przeciwników (groźny kandydat): 
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item pierwszy na lewo $c_L$ i pierwszy na prawo $c_R$ takich, że $score(c_?) \geq score(p)$
        \item powtarzamy dla kolejnych lewych $c_{L_{i}}$ takich, że $score(c_{L_{i}}) > score(c_{L_{i-1}})$
        \item analogicznie dla prawych $c_{R_{i}}$ takich, że $score(c_{R_{i}}) > score(c_{R_{i-1}})$
    \end{enumerate}
    \item usuwanie głosów - zaczynając od najbliższego prawego przeciwnika $c_R$ (dla lewych - odbicie lustrzane)
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item notacja $v$: $b(v)$ początek przedziału (lewy) i $e(v)$ koniec przedziały (prawy)
        \item wybieramy głosujących $\widehat{V}$ takich że $\{v \in V | \: b(v) \in (p,c_R]\}$
        \item sortujemy $\widehat{V}$ malejąco po $e(v)$ (od najbardziej prawego końca przedziału)
        \item usuwamy $scoreDelta(p, c_R) + 1$ głosów
    \end{enumerate}
    \item powtarzamy do skutu, przeliczając aprobaty co iterację
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{AV-CC-DV for Candidate Range = VI = column C1P = $r_v=0$}

Algorithm from \cite{control-cr-how-hard-magiera-faliszewski}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item notacja CR dla $c$: $v_{f(c)}$ początek przedziału (lewy/górny) i $v_{e(c)}$ koniec przedziału (prawy/dolny)
    \item kandydat $p$
    \item Głosujący - wybieramy głosujących $V' = \{v \in V | \: p \notin A(v)\}$ 
    \item Przeciwnicy -  wybieramy kandydatów $\widehat{C} = \{c \in C \setminus \{p\} | \: scoreDelta(c,p) \geq 0\}$
    \item Usuwanie głosów 
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item zaczynając od przeciwnika $c_N$ takiego, że $v_{e(c_N)}$ jest minimalne
        \item usuwamy $deltaScore(c_N,p)+1$ głosów, zaczynając od końca przedziału $v_{e(c_{N})}$
    \end{enumerate} 
    \item powtarzamy do skutu, przeliczając $V'$ i $\widehat{C}$ co iterację
\end{enumerate}

Notation used in the algorithm and later in the proofs: \\
$x_a$, $r_a$ — point and radius \\ 
$b(a) = x_a - r_a$ — range beginning (left)\\
$e(a) = x_a + r_a$ — range end (right) \\
$p$ — candidate chosen to be a winner \\
$\widehat{C} = \{c \in C \setminus \{p\} | \: scoreDelta(c,p) \geq 0\}$ — opponents (dangerous candidates) \\
$V' = \{v \in V | \: p \notin A(v)\}$ — potential voters to delete \\

\noindent We use “smart” greedy strategy for voter deletion:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We pick a leftmost opponent $c_L$ that is the one with the minimal $b(c_L)$
    \item We consider only voters who approve of $c_L$ that is, $\widehat{V} = \{v \in V' | \: c_L \in A(v)\}$
    \item We need to delete $deltaScore(c_L,p)+1$ rightmost voters from $\widehat{V}$
        that is the ones with the maximal $e(v)$, if it's not possible we fail
    \item We repeat the above steps until we succeed, recalculating $\widehat{C}$ and $\widehat{V}$ after each iteration
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Sketch Proof - CR case}
We will show how the VCR algorithm is equivalent to the CR one from \cite{control-cr-how-hard-magiera-faliszewski}.
Having voters in CR order, that is, they are still represented by a point $x_v$ on the $\mathbb{R}$ line but have radius $0$.
Example in Figure \ref{fig:cr-cc-dv}.

    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{ccP-p0.png}
    \centering
    \caption{Temporary Image - CR Election}
    \label{fig:cr-cc-dv}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Choosing opponents}
In the CR algorithm, we choose candidates to process, ordered by $v_{e(c)}$ ascending.
We can see that it is equivalent to picking the leftmost ($min(b(c))$) candidate from the set of opponents $\widehat{C}$ in the VCR algorithm,
because it will also have the minimal $v_{e(c)}$.

There is a special case of "contained candidate", for which the above statement is not true.
In Section \ref{sss:contained-candidate}, we explain in detail why it is irrelevant for the overall correctness of the algorithm.

\subsubsection{Voter deletion}
Now, once we now how to pick an opponent $c_L$, we will show that choosing voters is equivalent as well.
Notion of the beginning/end of the interval in the CR order is equivalent to the left/right end of the range in the VCR setting.
Both algorithms try to delete $scoreDelta(p,c_L) + 1$ voters,
CR one starts deleting voters from the end of interval, while
VCR does virtually the same by deleting the rightmost voter first ($max(e(v))$).

\subsection{Sketch Proof - VR case}
We will show how the VCR algorithm is equivalent to the VR one from \cite{control-sp-shield-faliszewski-hemaspaandra}.
Having candidates in VR order, that is, they are still represented by a point $x_c$ on the $\mathbb{R}$ line but have radius $0$.
Example in Figure \ref{fig:vr-cc-dv}.

\subsubsection{Choosing opponents}
VR algorithm picks dangerous candidates to the left and right of candidate $p$,
however later it proceeds only with defeating the right ones.
Once finished with them, it does the "mirror flip" of the universe to process candidates on the left side.
Even though the VCR algorithm picks leftmost candidate, we will show it is equivalent to the VR strategy
under some basic assumptions.

Following example in Figure \ref{fig:vr-cc-dv},
let's assume that candidate $p$ always will be the first candidate in the VR order,
which is equivalent to being the leftmost in the VCR order.
Then we can see that the VCR algorithm strategy of
choosing the leftmost candidate as an opponent
will be the same as the VR algorithm choosing the first candidate on the right side of $p$.
In the example it would be candidate $c_1$.

This assumption is correct because, having candidates in order $[c_L, \: p, \: c_R]$,
if there was a voter $v$ who approved of $c_L$ and $c_R$ it would also have to approve of $p$ (because of the VR property),
so we could not delete $v$ anyway.

\subsubsection{Voter deletion}
The reasoning here is very similar to the one in the voter deletion part of the CR proof.
Both algorithms try to delete $scoreDelta(c,p) + 1$ voters. 
VR algorithm starts deleting voters from the end of interval ($max(e(v))$).

\subsection{Sketch Proof - VCR (not C1P) case}

\begin{definition}
    We introduce an alternative representation of an agent in a VCR profile.
    Every agent $a$ is represented by the point $x_a$ and the radius $r_a$.
    We define $a$'s range beginning as $b(a) = x_a - r_a$, and $a$'s range end as $e(a) = x_a + r_a$. 
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
    Having a solution $S$ to the AV-CC-VD problem, which deletes $k$ voters.
    We define the score of this solution as, \[score(S) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} e(v_i) \]
    \textcolor{orange}{(WIP: Dlaczego możemy zdefiniować jakiś wynik, czy wynik jest po prostu równy k?)}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
    VCR-AV-CC-VD finds an optimal solution.
    \begin{proof}
        Let $S$ be the solution found by the VCR-AV-CC-VD algorithm, 
        and $S^{*}$ an optimal solution to the problem.
        We will prove that $S = S^{*}$ by contradiction.
        Assume $S \neq S^{*}$, therefore, $S - S^* \neq \emptyset$.
        Let $v$, be any voter in $S - S^*$.
        When $v$ was added to $S$, it was the rightmost voter ($\max(e(v))$).
        Since $S^*$ is optimal, then there must be some voter $x \in S^*$, such that $e(x) < e(v)$, because $v$ is the rightmost voter.
        Let $S^{*\prime} = S^* \cup \{v\} - \{x\}$, we know that $S^{*\prime}$ is a correct solution.
        \textcolor{orange}{(WIP: Dlaczego $S^{*\prime}$ jest poprawny? -
        Czy to właśnie tu musimy uzasadnić specjalny przpadek "wewnętrznych kandydatów"?
        Czy może być to jakiś osobny, wcześniej pokazany lemat?)}
        However, \[score(S^{*\prime}) = score(S^*) + e(v) - e(x) > score(S^*)\]
        contradicting that $S^*$ is an optimal solution.
        We have reached a contradiction, so our assumption must have been wrong.
        Thus $S = S^*$, hence $S$ is an optimal solution.
    \end{proof}
\end{proposition}

\subsubsection{Special case - contained candidates - alternative explanation}
Now we will show why our solution is correct in the general VCR setting, even when the profile does not have VR/CR property,
example in Figure \ref{fig:ncop-cc-dv}. The proof is split into two stages, similarly to the previous ones.
But first we will explain one special case of the election, that is worth examining.

In the VCR model it is possible that some candidates have their interval fully contained in the other's interval. 
Every voter who would approve the smaller candidate would also approve the larger one, however not vice versa.
For this case, our strategy of choosing voters to delete might seem suboptimal, at first glance.

Stages 1 through 3 are trivial, in the subsequent stages we make the election more and more complex by adding new agents.
Turning our attention to Stage 4, now both candidates $c_1$ and $c_2$ are dangerous, with scores 2 and 1 respectively.
Candidate $p$ needs to defeat $c_2$ by one vote, and $c_1$ by two votes. 
In Stage 5 we add multiple more voters ... 

The core idea to solve our issue is understanding what types of the new votes we can add.
Let's consider only the voters who approve of $c_1$ and/or $c_2$.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A voter who approves only $c_1$ and not $c_2$,
    in this case, $scoreDelta(p,c_1)$ would increase by 1, and $scoreDelta(p,c_2)$ would stay the same.
    \item A voter who approves both $c_1$ and $c_2$, this time,
    $scoreDelta(p,c_1)$ and $scoreDelta(p,c_2)$ would increase equally by 1.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Special case - contained candidates} \label{sss:contained-candidate}
In the VCR model it is possible that some candidates have their interval fully contained in the other's interval. 
Every voter who would approve the smaller candidate would also approve the larger one, however not vice versa.
For this case, our strategy of choosing voters to delete might seem suboptimal, at first glance. 

The example from Figure \ref{fig:contained-candidate} illustrates why we do not have to worry about this special case.
Every contained voter will have as much or less total votes than the larger candidate, 
he/she might not even be a dangerous candidate at all. 
Now we see that in the process of defeating the larger candidate we will automatically defeat the smaller one.

\subsubsection{Choosing opponents}
We consider only dangerous candidates (opponents), that is the ones with score higher or equal to the score of $p$.
We process opponents one by one, starting with the one who has the leftmost beginning of the range, i.e., $min(b(c))$.
After each iteration, we recompute the set of dangerous candidates.
Thanks to this we are sure that all the steps we perform are necessary to find a solution (if it exists), hence optimal.

\subsubsection{Voter deletion}
Having currently processed opponent $c_L$, we consider only the voters who approve of him/her and do not approve of $p$,
that is the voters whose range beginning is inside the candidate's range $\{v \in V' \: | \: b(v) \in [b(c_L), e(c_L)]\}$.
We need to delete $scoreDelta(c_L,p) + 1$ voters to defeat the opponent.
By picking the voters in order of the rightmost end of the range, i.e., $v_R = max(e(v))$,
we try to maximize the number of other candidates who might be in the range $[b(v_R), e(v_R)]$.
We don't have to worry about any candidate to the left,
because of our leftmost candidate picking strategy, they are already defeated.
This strategy ensures the solution is optimal if it exists.

\section{AV-CC-AV for the VCR profiles}
Our strategy is based on the solution from \cite{control-cr-how-hard-magiera-faliszewski}.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Add all $v \in W$ to the election
    \item Is $p$ the winner?
    \item Try to delete $k' = |W| - k$ voters 
\end{enumerate}
